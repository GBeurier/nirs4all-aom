\title{Operator-Adaptive Partial Least Squares for Near-Infrared Spectroscopy:\\
Unified AOM/POP Selection, Covariance-Space SIMPLS, and PLS-DA}

\author[mainaff]{Gr\'egory Beurier}
\ead{gregory.beurier@cirad.fr}

\author[mainaff]{nirs4all contributors}

\address[mainaff]{nirs4all open-source project, France}

\begin{abstract}
Near-infrared spectroscopy~(NIRS) calibration depends on a long
preprocessing chain that is typically searched as an external
hyper\-parameter grid before fitting Partial Least Squares regression
(\PLS). We present a unified \emph{Operator-Adaptive \PLS} framework in
which spectral preprocessing is moved inside the latent extraction loop.
Every preprocessing step is treated as a strict linear spectral operator
$\Amat\in\R^{p\times p}$ acting by $\Xmat\Amat^\top$, and the cross-
covariance identity
$(\Xmat\Amat^\top)^\top \Ymat = \Amat\,\Xmat^\top \Ymat$ is exploited
to evaluate candidate operators directly in covariance space.
\textbf{The framework is built on top of, and is parity-validated
against, \nirsfourall's deployed \texttt{AOMPLSRegressor}}: an
automated regression test verifies numerical equivalence (RMSEP
difference under
$10^{-4}$, identical selected component count $k^\star$ and operator
index $b^\star$) on every dataset of an 11-split parity cohort. This
production parity is the foundation for the empirical benchmark and
for the research extensions we layer on top. We describe the
production-aligned
\NIPALS-adjoint flow (the four-step
$g=\Amat^\top\!c \rightarrow \hat\wvec=g/\|g\| \rightarrow a_w=\Amat\hat\wvec \rightarrow \wvec=a_w/\|a_w\|$
update that is the parity-critical operation), give \SIMPLS engines in
both materialized and covariance space, and add three research
extensions that all reuse the parity-validated \AOM core: an
\emph{Active Superblock} multi-view mode with covariance screening and
Frobenius balancing, an \emph{operator explorer} that beam-searches
composition chains in covariance space, and \emph{deep banks} of
order-3 and order-4 strict-linear chains. The framework is also
extended to \PLSDA with class-balanced latent coding
$Y_{ic}=1/\sqrt{\pi_c}$ and a leakage-safe logistic-regression
calibrator on the latent scores. On a 57-dataset regression cohort
joined to the published TabPFN/NIRS reference results, the framework
benchmarks 128 variants under one estimator: the deployed production
\AOM-\PLS wins on 29/57 datasets (median RMSEP ratio $0.999$ vs
\PLS); the strongest variant overall is
\texttt{ASLS-AOM-compact-cv5}, an asymmetric-least-squares baseline
correction piped in front of the 9-operator compact bank with 5-fold
cross-validation replacing the deterministic 20\% holdout, which
reaches median ratio $\mathbf{0.960}$ with $\mathbf{42/57}$ wins, a
4\% median improvement and 13 additional dataset wins over the
deployed reference. \textbf{This new champion ties or beats three of
the four reference baselines from the TabPFN/NIRS paper at the
median}: TabPFN-Raw (0.994 median ratio, 30/57 wins), Catboost
(0.998, 28/56), and CNN (0.987, 28/51); only TabPFN-opt with its
per-dataset hyperparameter optimisation remains ahead (1.117, 18/57).
Runners-up \texttt{ASLS-AOM-response-dedup-cv3} (median $0.964$, 37
wins) and \texttt{ASLS-AOM-family-pruned-cv3} (median $0.964$, 38
wins) demonstrate that a response-cosine pruned 46-op bank or a
family-pruned 15-op bank, both with ASLS preprocessing and CV-3
selection, recover most of the headline gain. We do not claim
novelty for the \AOM-\PLS algorithm itself, which is the deployed
\nirsfourall implementation; we claim a parity-validated framework
wrapper that lets multi-view operator selection, beam-searched
composition chains, alternative selection criteria, non-linear
preprocessing baselines, and asymmetric-least-squares baseline
correction be evaluated against that production reference under one
mathematical contract.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%% =====================================================================

Near-infrared spectroscopy~(NIRS) is widely used for non-destructive
quantification and discrimination of organic mixtures, with applications
in agronomy, food science, and pharmaceuticals~\cite{williams2007implementation,
rinnan2009review}. Predictive performance depends not only on the
calibration model but also on a sequence of spectral preprocessing
operations: scatter correction, baseline removal, smoothing, and
derivatives. In Partial Least Squares regression~(\PLS,
\cite{wold1975soft, geladi1986partial, wold2001pls}), each candidate
preprocessing pipeline gives a different latent decomposition, hence a
different prediction. Practitioners typically grid-search a subset of
the Cartesian product of preprocessing choices, with cross-validation
selecting the best pipeline for each new dataset.

The combinatorics of preprocessing search are uncomfortably large: a
realistic NIRS workflow may try $\mathcal{O}(10^2)$ pipelines per
calibration. The decisions are also opaque. Two analysts with the same
data and the same operator inventory can converge to different
pipelines, and standard reporting practices do not make those choices
auditable. From a mathematical standpoint, treating preprocessing as an
external hyper\-parameter ignores the fact that strictly linear
operators commute cleanly with the cross-covariance that drives the
\PLS extraction.

This paper asks a single methodological question: \emph{can preprocessing
be moved from an external grid into the \PLS extraction itself, as a
selection problem on a bank of strict linear operators?} The answer is
a family of \emph{Operator-Adaptive \PLS} algorithms. Instead of fitting
many independent \PLS models on different transformed matrices, we
maintain a single estimator that selects, possibly per latent
component, the best operator from a fixed bank.

Two earlier ideas in the chemometrics community inform this work. The
\emph{Adaptive Operator-Mixture} (\AOM) idea selects one global
preprocessing among several candidates inside the \PLS routine, using a
hold-out or covariance criterion. The \emph{Per-Operator Per-component}
(\POP) idea allows the operator to vary across latent components,
turning preprocessing into a per-step decision. Both are deployed in the
\nirsfourall library as \verb|AOMPLSRegressor| and \verb|POPPLSRegressor|
under \verb|nirs4all.operators.models|~\cite{aompls2025prior, nirs4all2025}.
The \nirsfourall \AOM-\PLS estimator is therefore the
\emph{deployed reference}, not a competitor: anything we propose here
must reproduce its predictions within floating-point tolerance before its mathematical
properties can be discussed in print.

This paper takes that constraint seriously. We provide a parity-validated
re-implementation of \AOM-\PLS as a \emph{framework} (\verb|bench/AOM_v0/aompls/|)
with a regression test that asserts floating-point equivalence with
\verb|nirs4all.operators.models.sklearn.aom_pls.AOMPLSRegressor|
on every dataset of an 11-split parity cohort (RMSEP diff $<10^{-4}$,
identical $k^\star$, identical $b^\star$; tests in
\verb|bench/AOM_v0/tests/test_parity_with_production.py|). On top of
that parity-validated core we then add covariance-space \SIMPLS, a
multi-view Active Superblock mode, an operator explorer with deterministic
beam search, and deep banks of order-3 / order-4 strict-linear chains.
The framework is also extended to \PLSDA with class-balanced coding.

The contributions of this work are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,label=(\arabic*)]
  \item A \textbf{parity-validated re-implementation} of \nirsfourall's
        \texttt{AOMPLSRegressor} (Section~\ref{sec:bit-parity}). Strict
        numerical equivalence is verified on 11/11 datasets of a regression
        cohort and is guarded by an automated test. The six
        parity-critical implementation details are documented and
        recorded as the reproducibility surface of the benchmark
        (Section~\ref{sec:bit-parity}).
  \item A \textbf{unified mathematical formulation}
        (Section~\ref{sec:framework}) that places standard \PLS, \AOM,
        \POP, and superblock multi-view \PLS on a common operator-bank
        contract with the central identity
        $(\Xmat\Amat^\top)^\top \Ymat = \Amat\,\Xmat^\top \Ymat$.
  \item \textbf{Algorithm specifications}
        (Section~\ref{sec:algorithms}): the production-aligned
        \NIPALS-adjoint four-step flow $g=\Amat^\top c\to\hat\wvec\to a_w=\Amat\hat\wvec\to\wvec$,
        a covariance-space \SIMPLS engine that follows from the
        operator identity, and the strict-\SIMPLS contrast
        ($\Amat^\top\Amat c$) that disagrees with the production
        reference for non-commuting compositions.
  \item Three \textbf{research extensions} of the parity-validated
        \AOM core (Sections~\ref{sec:algo-active-superblock},
        \ref{sec:algo-explorer}): an Active Superblock multi-view
        engine that covariance-screens the bank and balances retained
        blocks by Frobenius norm, an operator explorer that beam-
        searches composition chains in covariance space, and deep
        banks (\texttt{deep3} = 116 ops, \texttt{deep4} = 132 ops)
        that extend the production 100-operator default with order-3
        and order-4 chains.
  \item A \textbf{class-balanced \PLSDA extension}
        (Section~\ref{sec:classification}) with $Y_{ic}=1/\sqrt{\pi_c}$
        latent coding and a leakage-safe logistic-regression calibrator
        on the latent scores, with a temperature-scaled softmax
        fallback.
  \item A \textbf{leakage-safe experimental protocol}
        (Section~\ref{sec:protocol}) that joins our variants to the
        published TabPFN/NIRS regression cohort and to a classification
        cohort assembled from open NIRS sources, together with
        signed-difference reporting against \PLS, TabPFN-Raw, and
        TabPFN-opt.
\end{enumerate}

What is \emph{not} claimed: we do not claim algorithmic novelty for
\AOM-\PLS itself --- the deployed \nirsfourall implementation is the
reference and our framework reproduces it within floating-point
tolerance. We do not claim
universal superiority over TabPFN~\cite{hollmann2025tabpfn,
grinsztajn2025tabpfn25} or over modern deep tabular models;
generalisation conclusions are limited to the measured cohort. What we
do claim is that the strongest operator-adaptive \PLS variants win
against standard \PLS on the majority of NIRS regression splits in our
cohort, and that production \AOM-\PLS, compact-bank \AOM, Active
Superblock, and the operator explorer can be compared under one
auditable contract on the same splits.

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related}
%% =====================================================================

\subsection{Partial Least Squares: \NIPALS and \SIMPLS}
\PLS extracts latent variables that maximise covariance between
input matrix $\Xmat\in\R^{n\times p}$ and target matrix
$\Ymat\in\R^{n\times q}$. The classical \NIPALS
algorithm~\cite{wold1975soft, geladi1986partial, wold2001pls}
deflates $\Xmat$ and $\Ymat$ iteratively, alternating between the
weight $\wvec_a\propto \Xmat^\top \Ymat\,\cdot$, the score
$\tvec_a=\Xmat\wvec_a$, and the loading
$\pvec_a=\Xmat^\top \tvec_a/\|\tvec_a\|^2$. \SIMPLS~\cite{dejong1993simpls}
reformulates the extraction so that all weights are mutually orthogonal
and produced from successive orthogonalisations of the cross-covariance
$\Smat=\Xmat^\top \Ymat$, without deflating $\Xmat$ at each step. The
two algorithms produce numerically equivalent regression coefficients
when the scoring objective is shared~\cite{dejong1993simpls,
martens2001reliable, martens1989multivariate}. Cross-validation for
choosing the number of components follows~\cite{wold1978cross,
krzanowski1987cv}.

\subsection{NIRS preprocessing}
Spectral preprocessing addresses scatter, baseline drift, and
overlapping bands~\cite{rinnan2009review}. Common operators include
Savitzky-Golay smoothing and derivatives~\cite{savitzky1964smoothing},
Norris-Williams gap derivatives~\cite{norris1976influence}, polynomial
detrending, finite differences, and Whittaker
smoothing~\cite{eilers2003perfect}. Multiplicative scatter correction
(MSC)~\cite{geladi1985linearization} and standard normal variate
(SNV)~\cite{barnes1989standard} are very common but, importantly, they
are \emph{not strictly linear} as fixed-matrix operators on row spectra:
their parameters depend on the row being transformed. We therefore
exclude SNV/MSC from the \emph{strict} operator bank used by the
covariance identity and discuss them in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{PLS-DA}
PLS for discrimination encodes class labels as a one-hot or
class-balanced indicator matrix and recovers latent variables that
maximise covariance with class
membership~\cite{barker2003plsda, indahl2007twostep, brereton2014plsda}.
Calibration to probabilities is needed if a downstream task requires
log-loss or expected calibration error (ECE), since raw \PLSDA scores
are unconstrained. Standard remedies are logistic regression on latent
scores, Platt scaling~\cite{platt1999probabilistic}, or temperature
scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration}.

\subsection{Tabular foundation models and learnable kernels}
TabPFN~\cite{hollmann2023tabpfn, hollmann2025tabpfn,
grinsztajn2025tabpfn25} is a transformer-based prior-fitted network
that solves small tabular problems competitively with classical
\PLS-Ridge baselines. Reference NIRS evaluations
(\verb|bench/tabpfn_paper/master_results.csv|) report TabPFN-Raw and
TabPFN-opt root-mean-squared errors on a curated set of public NIRS
calibrations, alongside CNN, CatBoost, \PLS, and Ridge baselines. Recent
chemometric work also explores learnable spectral
kernels~\cite{aompls2024fckpls} that move part of the preprocessing
into trainable convolutions. Operator-Adaptive \PLS occupies a
different point in design space: it is non-deep, bank-restricted,
auditable, and produces an interpretable \emph{operator path} whose
elements are familiar chemometric operators.

\subsection{AOM/POP precursors and the deployed reference}
The Adaptive Operator-Mixture and Per-Operator Per-component ideas
appeared in the \nirsfourall project as \verb|AOMPLSRegressor| and
\verb|POPPLSRegressor| under \verb|nirs4all.operators.models|. The
\nirsfourall \verb|AOMPLSRegressor| is the \emph{deployed} reference: it
ships with a default 100-operator bank (1 Identity, 32 Savitzky-Golay
family, 3 detrend, 64 composed chains), an internal hold-out selection
on \verb|RandomState(42)|, and a hard-gate \NIPALS-adjoint extraction
loop. The present work treats it as the source of truth: our framework
is parity-validated against that estimator on a parity cohort
(Section~\ref{sec:bit-parity}). We then formalise the operator-bank
contract under the covariance identity, add a covariance-space \SIMPLS
engine for the cases where it is well defined, generalise the framework
to \PLSDA, provide PyTorch backends, and add three research extensions
(Active Superblock, operator explorer, deep banks). Earlier orthogonal
projection-to-latent-structures work~\cite{trygg2002orthogonal} and
multi-block \PLS~\cite{westerhuis1998analysis} are conceptually related
but address different problems (variance partitioning rather than
preprocessing selection).

\section{Operator-Adaptive PLS framework}\label{sec:framework}
%% =====================================================================

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} summarises the framework: a bank of strict
linear spectral operators feeds a selection policy, which feeds the
\PLS core (\NIPALS or \SIMPLS, materialized or covariance-space),
which feeds the regression and \PLSDA tasks. Diagnostics record the
operator path so every preprocessing decision is auditable.

\centering

(\textsc{none}, \textsc{global}, \textsc{per\_component},
\textsc{soft}, \textsc{superblock}), the \PLS core operating in
covariance space via the central identity
$(\Xmat\Amat^\top)^\top\Ymat=\Amat\Xmat^\top\Ymat$, and the regression

\subsection{Notation and the operator contract}
Let $\Xmat\in\R^{n\times p}$ collect $n$ centered row spectra of length
$p$, and let $\Ymat\in\R^{n\times q}$ be the centered response matrix
($q=1$ for univariate regression, $q=K$ for multi-class \PLSDA). A
\emph{strict linear spectral operator} is a matrix $\Amat\in\R^{p\times
p}$ that acts on row spectra by right-multiplication of its transpose:
\begin{equation}
\Xmat_b \;=\; \Xmat\,\Amat_b^\top.
\label{eq:operator-action}
\end{equation}
Strict linearity is essential. Operators with row-dependent parameters
(SNV, MSC) are \emph{not} strictly linear in this sense and are
excluded from the operator bank used by the covariance identity. The
operators supported here are Savitzky-Golay smoothers and derivatives,
Norris-Williams gap derivatives, polynomial detrend projections, finite
differences, Whittaker smoothers, identity, and arbitrary linear
compositions thereof.

A \emph{bank} is a finite set of strict linear operators
$\mathcal{B}=\{\Amat_1,\dots,\Amat_B\}$ where $\Amat_1=\identity$ is the
identity (auto-prepended; the production reference does the same). We
expose five named presets:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \texttt{compact}, $|\mathcal{B}|=9$: identity, two SG smoothers,
        two SG first-derivatives, one SG second-derivative, two detrend
        projections, one finite-difference.
  \item \texttt{default}, $|\mathcal{B}|=100$: an exact 1:1 mirror of
        the production \verb|nirs4all.default_operator_bank|. Composition:
        1 identity, 32 savgol-family (SG smoothing/derivatives across
        windows 11/15/21/31/41 and polyorders 2/3, two finite differences,
        eight Norris-Williams gap-derivative variants), 3 detrend
        projections (degree 0, 1, 2), and 64 composed operators (every
        savgol-family entry composed with detrend degree 0 and degree 1).
  \item \texttt{extended}, $|\mathcal{B}|=103$: \texttt{default} plus
        three Whittaker smoothers ($\lambda\in\{10, 10^3, 10^5\}$).
  \item \texttt{deep3}, $|\mathcal{B}|=116$: \texttt{default} plus 16
        order-3 chains (smoothing $\to$ derivative $\to$ detrend) over
        a small set of stage primitives.
  \item \texttt{deep4}, $|\mathcal{B}|=132$: \texttt{deep3} plus 16
        order-4 chains (detrend $\to$ smoothing $\to$ derivative
        $\to$ detrend) with the canonicalisation rule that consecutive
        detrends must have different degrees.
\end{itemize}
The \texttt{default} bank is the parity-validated bank used by every
\verb|nirs4all-AOM-PLS| run reported in this paper. The \texttt{deep3}
and \texttt{deep4} presets are research-only banks for ablation
studies of how much extra capacity a deeper composition grammar
provides.

\subsection{The central covariance identity}
The cornerstone of the framework is the identity
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
(\Xmat\Amat^\top)^\top \Ymat \;=\; \Amat\,\Xmat^\top \Ymat
\;}
\label{eq:central-identity}
\end{equation}
which holds for any strict linear $\Amat$. Equation~\eqref{eq:central-identity}
states that the cross-covariance of the transformed matrix
$\Xmat_b=\Xmat\Amat^\top$ with $\Ymat$ equals the operator $\Amat$
applied to the cross-covariance $\Smat=\Xmat^\top\Ymat$ of the original
matrix. Two practical consequences follow:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,label=(\arabic*)]
  \item Operator selection can be \emph{evaluated} in covariance space
        via $\Smat_b=\Amat\,\Smat$ without ever materialising
        $\Xmat\Amat^\top$. This is the basis of the covariance-space
        \SIMPLS engine in Section~\ref{sec:algo-simpls-cov}.
  \item Effective \emph{regression weights} in the \emph{original}
        spectral space follow the adjoint rule
        $\zvec=\Amat^\top\rvec$, where $\rvec$ is a weight vector
        living in the transformed space. This lets us combine
        per-component operators that vary by component while keeping
        the final coefficient $\Bmat$ defined on the original scale.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Selection policies}
Let $\mathcal{B}$ be a bank and $K$ the number of components. A
\emph{selection policy} chooses a sequence of operators
$(\Amat^{(1)},\dots,\Amat^{(K)})\in\mathcal{B}^K$. We support six
policies:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item[\textsc{none}.] $\mathcal{B}=\{\identity\}$: standard
  \PLS. Recovered as a degenerate selection.
  \item[\textsc{global} (\AOM).] One operator $\Amat^\star$ used for all
  components: $\Amat^{(a)}=\Amat^\star$ for $a=1,\dots,K$.
  Selection criterion is computed once on the bank.
  \item[\textsc{per\_component} (\POP).] Operator chosen independently at
  each component, using the residualised cross-covariance after
  $a-1$ components have been deflated.
  \item[\textsc{soft} (experimental).] Convex combinations
  $\Amat^{(a)}=\sum_b \lambda_b^{(a)} \Amat_b$ with $\lambda^{(a)}\in
  \Delta^{B-1}$. We discuss in Section~\ref{sec:limitations} why this
  policy collapses to hard selection under covariance scoring and
  is therefore exposed only as a research interface.
  \item[\textsc{superblock}.] Concatenate every transformed view into a
  wide matrix $\Xmat_{\mathrm{sup}}=[\Xmat\Amat_1^\top,\dots,
  \Xmat\Amat_B^\top]$ and run standard \SIMPLS on it. The wide-space
  weights are mapped back to original-space coefficients before
  prediction. This is the multi-view baseline
  of~\cite{westerhuis1998analysis}.
  \item[\textsc{active\_superblock}.] First select an active subset of
  the bank by covariance score and response-cosine diversity, scale
  every retained block by Frobenius norm, then run \SIMPLS on the
  resulting balanced wide matrix. Coefficients are mapped back to
  original-space (Section~\ref{sec:algo-active-superblock}).
\end{description}

The bank itself can be a fixed preset (\texttt{compact},
\texttt{default} -- 100 ops, matches the production deployment exactly --
\texttt{extended}, \texttt{deep3}, or \texttt{deep4}) or the output of an
\emph{operator explorer} that runs a deterministic beam search over
chains of strict-linear primitives (Section~\ref{sec:algo-explorer}).

\subsection{Selection criteria}
Five criteria can be plugged into any of the policies above:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Covariance.} The greedy \PLS score
  $\|\rvec_b^\top \Smat_b\|$ at each step. Cheap, but
  selection-noisy on small datasets.
  \item \textbf{CV (default).} Inner $k$-fold cross-validation root
  mean squared error of prediction (RMSEP) on the training fold.
  \item \textbf{Approximate PRESS.} Leave-one-sample-out PRESS based on
  the closed-form leverage~\cite{krzanowski1987cv}.
  \item \textbf{Hybrid.} Convex blend of covariance and CV scores,
  trading speed against stability.
  \item \textbf{Hold-out.} Single internal split scored on RMSEP.
  Reserved for cohort-level smoke tests.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Orthogonalisation modes}
When operators vary by component (\POP and \textsc{soft}), the
transformed-space orthogonalisation that \SIMPLS uses by default is
no longer correct: each new component would be orthogonalised against
loadings from a different transformed space. We therefore expose two
modes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{transformed.} Default for \textsc{global} and
  \textsc{none}. \SIMPLS deflations and \NIPALS deflations occur in
  the operator-transformed space.
  \item \textbf{original.} Required for \POP and \textsc{soft}.
  Effective weights $\zvec_a=\Amat^{(a)\,\top} \rvec_a$ are mapped
  back to the original spectral space, so all loadings and scores
  live on a single coordinate system. This is the default whenever
  the policy admits varying operators.
\end{itemize}
The estimator exposes \verb|orthogonalization="auto"| which selects the
correct mode from the policy.

\subsection{Effective weights and prediction}
After selection, the estimator returns matrices
$\Zmat\in\R^{p\times K}$ (effective original-space weights),
$\Pmat\in\R^{p\times K}$ (loadings) and $\Qmat\in\R^{q\times K}$
(target loadings). The regression coefficient is
\begin{equation}
\Bmat \;=\; \Zmat\,(\Pmat^\top\Zmat)^{+}\,\Qmat^\top,
\label{eq:beta}
\end{equation}
and predictions on a new spectrum $\xvec_\star$ are
\begin{equation}
\hat{\yvec}_\star \;=\; (\xvec_\star - \bar{\xvec})^\top \Bmat
+ \bar{\yvec},
\label{eq:predict}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\xvec}$ and $\bar{\yvec}$ are the training means.
Equation~\eqref{eq:beta} is identical in form to the standard \PLS
formula; the operator-adaptive nature is hidden inside the construction
of $\Zmat$. This means downstream tooling (e.g.\ stacking, retraining,
SHAP wrappers) sees the model as a regular linear regressor.

\section{Algorithms}\label{sec:algorithms}
%% =====================================================================

\subsection{NIPALS materialized (reference)}
\label{sec:algo-nipals-mat}
The materialized \NIPALS engine simply applies each operator
explicitly to obtain $\Xmat_b=\Xmat\Amat_b^\top$, then runs standard
\NIPALS on each $\Xmat_b$. With per-component selection, the engine
applies a possibly different operator at each step and re-builds
$\Xmat_b$ from the deflated residual. This engine serves as a slow,
self-evident reference for unit tests; numerical equivalence with
the fast engines is asserted up to round-off.

\subsection{NIPALS adjoint, production-aligned (fast)}
\label{sec:algo-nipals-adj}
The adjoint engine never builds $\Xmat\Amat^\top$. It implements the
exact four-step weight update used inside the production
\verb|nirs4all.AOMPLSRegressor| --- this update is the parity-critical
operation for the numerical equivalence verified in
Section~\ref{sec:bit-parity}. At step $a$ on residuals
$(\Xmat_a,\Ymat_a)$ with cross-covariance $\Smat_a=\Xmat_a^\top\Ymat_a$:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item For each candidate $b$, evaluate the operator-applied
        cross-covariance $\Smat_a^{(b)}=\Amat_b\Smat_a$ and score
        it under the policy-induced criterion.
  \item Select index $b^\star$.
  \item Take the dominant left-singular vector $\cvec_a$ of
        $\Smat_a^{(b^\star)}$ (the column itself for PLS1).
  \item \textbf{Adjoint weight update (parity-critical):}
        \begin{align*}
        \gvec      &\,=\, \Amat_{b^\star}^\top \cvec_a, \\
        \hat{\wvec}_a &\,=\, \gvec / \|\gvec\|, \\
        \avec_w    &\,=\, \Amat_{b^\star}\,\hat{\wvec}_a, \\
        \wvec_a    &\,=\, \avec_w / \|\avec_w\|.
        \end{align*}
  \item Compute the score $\tvec_a=\Xmat_a\hat{\wvec}_a$, the loading
        $\pvec_a=\Xmat_a^\top\tvec_a/\|\tvec_a\|^2$, the target loading
        $\qvec_a$, and deflate
        $\Xmat_{a+1}=\Xmat_a-\tvec_a\pvec_a^\top$,
        $\Ymat_{a+1}=\Ymat_a-\tvec_a\qvec_a^\top$.
\end{enumerate}
The four-step adjoint sub-block in step (4) is non-negotiable: any
shortcut --- e.g.\ collapsing it to $\wvec\propto\Amat^\top\cvec$ or to
$\wvec\propto\Amat^\top\Amat\,\cvec$ --- breaks parity with the deployed
estimator. The cost is $\mathcal{O}(np)$ per operator candidate (versus
$\mathcal{O}(np^2)$ for the materialized form when $\Amat$ is dense),
and the engine reproduces the production \verb|AOMPLSRegressor| RMSEPs
within floating-point tolerance on every parity-cohort dataset
(Section~\ref{sec:bit-parity}).

\paragraph{Strict-\SIMPLS contrast.}
A strict \SIMPLS reading of operator-aware extraction would use the
weight $\wvec\propto\Amat^\top\Amat\,\cvec$ (the gradient of
$\|\Amat\Smat\|$ on $\Smat$ is $\Amat^\top\Amat\Smat$). For symmetric or
anti-symmetric operators ($\Amat^\top\!=\pm\Amat$ up to sign) this
produces the same prediction as the four-step adjoint flow, but for
general non-commuting compositions in the production 100-operator bank
(notably composed savgol-then-detrend operators and Norris-Williams
gap-derivative kernels) it does not. This is why the \emph{deployed}
estimator uses the four-step adjoint flow, and why our parity test
asserts the four-step flow rather than a literal $\Amat^\top\Amat\cvec$
update.

\subsection{SIMPLS materialized}
\label{sec:algo-simpls-mat}
For \textsc{global} \AOM, \SIMPLS\ \cite{dejong1993simpls} is run on
the explicitly transformed matrix $\Xmat\Amat_b^\top$. Selection
chooses the bank index minimising the chosen criterion. For \POP,
\SIMPLS is started in the transformed space of $\Amat^{(1)}$ and
continued by changing the active operator at each step. The
transformed-space orthogonalisation is preserved when all operators
agree (\textsc{global}) and switched to original-space when operators
differ (\POP, \textsc{soft}).

\subsection{SIMPLS covariance space (fast, identity-driven)}
\label{sec:algo-simpls-cov}
The covariance-space \SIMPLS engine is the natural consequence of
identity~\eqref{eq:central-identity}. For each component $a$:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item Maintain a single cross-covariance matrix $\Smat\in\R^{p\times
        q}$, initialised at $\Xmat^\top \Ymat$.
  \item For each candidate operator $\Amat_b$, compute
        $\Smat_b=\Amat_b \Smat$ (cheap, $\mathcal{O}(p^2 q)$ at most;
        $\mathcal{O}(pq)$ when $\Amat_b$ is banded).
  \item Pick $b^\star$ via the policy.
  \item Take the dominant left-singular vector $\rvec_a$ of
        $\Smat_{b^\star}$ and the corresponding right-singular
        vector $\qvec_a$.
  \item Compute the effective weight $\zvec_a=\Amat_{b^\star}^\top
        \rvec_a$ in the original space.
  \item Build the score $\tvec_a=\Xmat\zvec_a$, the loading
        $\pvec_a=\Xmat^\top\tvec_a/\|\tvec_a\|^2$, and update $\Smat$
        by removing the rank-one contribution along $\pvec_a$:
        $\Smat\leftarrow\Smat-\pvec_a(\pvec_a^\top\Smat)$.
\end{enumerate}
This engine is mathematically equivalent to the materialized \SIMPLS
in \textsc{global} mode and to the residualised \NIPALS in
\textsc{per\_component} mode. Equivalence is asserted in the unit
tests under tight tolerance.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\SIMPLS in covariance space with operator selection.}
\label{alg:simpls-cov}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} centered $\Xmat\in\R^{n\times p}$,
$\Ymat\in\R^{n\times q}$, bank $\mathcal{B}=\{\Amat_b\}$,
policy $\Pi$, criterion $\Phi$, max components $K$.
\State $\Smat\gets \Xmat^\top \Ymat$
\State $\Zmat,\Pmat,\Qmat\gets [\,]$
\For{$a=1,\dots,K$}
  \State For each $b$, evaluate $\Phi(\Amat_b\Smat,\Pi,a)$.
  \State $b^\star\gets \arg\min_b \Phi$ (or argmax for covariance).
  \State $(\rvec_a,\sigma_a,\qvec_a)\gets \mathrm{TopSVD}(\Amat_{b^\star}\Smat)$.
  \State $\zvec_a\gets \Amat_{b^\star}^\top \rvec_a$.
  \State $\tvec_a\gets \Xmat \zvec_a$,~$\pvec_a\gets
        \Xmat^\top \tvec_a/\|\tvec_a\|^2$.
  \State $\Smat\gets \Smat - \pvec_a(\pvec_a^\top\Smat)$.
  \State Append columns to $\Zmat,\Pmat,\Qmat$.
\EndFor
\State $\Bmat\gets \Zmat(\Pmat^\top\Zmat)^{+}\Qmat^\top$.
\State \textbf{Return} $\Bmat,\Zmat,\Pmat,\Qmat$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Superblock SIMPLS}
\label{sec:algo-superblock}
The superblock engine concatenates all transformed views and runs
standard \SIMPLS on the resulting wide matrix
$\Xmat_{\mathrm{sup}}\in\R^{n\times pB}$. The operator path is replaced
by a path of \emph{block} indices. The wide-space weight matrix
$\Zmat_{\mathrm{wide}}$ is mapped back to the original feature space by
$\Zmat_{\mathrm{orig}}[:,a] = \sum_b \alpha_b \Amat_b^\top
\Zmat_{\mathrm{wide},b}[:,a]$ so that the predictor accepts plain
$(n,p)$ matrices. This baseline is comparable to multi-block
\PLS~\cite{westerhuis1998analysis}.

\subsection{Active Superblock}
\label{sec:algo-active-superblock}
\emph{Active Superblock} fixes a known weakness of raw superblock: high-gain
operators (sharp derivatives, high-pass filters) dominate by amplitude.
The mode (i)~screens operators by covariance score $-\|\Amat_b\Smat\|_F$,
(ii)~prunes redundant responses by absolute response cosine
$\geq 0.98$, (iii)~normalises every retained block by
$\alpha_b=\sqrt{n}/\|\Xmat \Amat_b^\top\|_F$ (Frobenius scaling), and
(iv)~runs \SIMPLS on the diverse, balanced wide matrix. The original-space
mapping of the previous paragraph is reused to keep
$\Bmat\in\R^{p\times q}$. Active Superblock is the multi-view
counterpart of \AOM-\textsc{global}: it gives every retained operator a
voice but enforces diversity and balanced amplitude up front.

\subsection{Operator explorer}
\label{sec:algo-explorer}
The operator explorer turns the bank into a small \emph{candidate grammar}
of strict-linear primitives (Savitzky-Golay scale-space, Whittaker
$\lambda$-grid, Gaussian-derivative filters, detrend projections, finite
differences, Norris-Williams gap derivatives, fixed integer-pixel shifts)
and explores compositions by deterministic beam search in covariance
space. At depth $d$, every kept state expands by every grammar-allowed
primitive; the new state response is the operator-applied covariance
$\Amat_{\mathrm{new}}\,\Smat_{\mathrm{prev}}$; the score is
$-\|\mathrm{response}\|/\mathrm{gain}$ with a probe-based
gain proxy; states are kept if their response cosine to already-kept
states stays below 0.98 and family quotas are respected. The output of
the explorer is a small active bank that can be passed to any of the
selection policies (\AOM-\textsc{global}, \POP, Active Superblock).
The explorer never inspects validation data: the only training-side
input is $\Smat=\Xmat^\top \Ymat$ on the training fold.

\subsection{Soft mixture: caveat}
\label{sec:algo-soft}
The soft mixture replaces the hard $b^\star$ choice with a convex
combination $\Amat^{(a)}=\sum_b \lambda_b^{(a)} \Amat_b$, fitted by an
exponentiated-gradient step on a covariance objective. Under the
Cauchy-Schwarz inequality the covariance objective is convex on the
simplex and is maximised at a vertex (Supplement~A.3). In practice the
solver therefore degenerates to hard \textsc{global} selection.
\textsc{soft} is exposed only as a research interface; the headline
results are reported on hard policies.

\section{Classification and probability calibration}\label{sec:classification}
%% =====================================================================

We extend the framework to multi-class \PLSDA. Let $y_i\in\{c_1,\dots,c_K\}$
be the class label for the $i$-th sample and let $\pi_c$ be the
empirical class prior. The class-balanced one-hot encoding is
\begin{equation}
Y_{ic} \;=\; \begin{cases}
1/\sqrt{\pi_c}, & y_i = c, \\
0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:class-coding}
\end{equation}
Coding~\eqref{eq:class-coding} is a re-balanced version of standard
\PLSDA~\cite{barker2003plsda, indahl2007twostep} that brings minority
classes' contributions to the cross-covariance to comparable scale,
mirroring the class-balanced regression weighting popular in modern
practice. After encoding, the centered $\Ymat$ feeds the same engines
described in Section~\ref{sec:algorithms}.

\paragraph{Logistic-regression calibration.}
Raw \PLSDA scores are real-valued and unbounded. We attach a
multinomial logistic regression on the latent scores
$\Tmat=\Xmat\Zmat\in\R^{n\times K}$ as a fold-internal calibrator:
\begin{equation}
\hat{p}(c\mid\xvec_\star) \;=\; \softmax(\Wmat_{\mathrm{lr}}\,
(\Xmat_\star\Zmat) + \bvec_{\mathrm{lr}})_c,
\end{equation}
with \verb|class_weight="balanced"| and L2 regularisation. Crucially,
\emph{the calibrator is fitted only inside the training fold of every
selection / cross-validation split}, so the protocol remains
leakage-safe.

\paragraph{Temperature-scaled softmax fallback.}
If the logistic calibrator fails to converge, the fallback returns
$\hat{p}(c\mid\xvec_\star)=\softmax(\Tmat_\star/\tau)_c$ with a
temperature $\tau>0$ fitted by minimising training-fold log-loss. This
keeps the estimator usable in degenerate regimes where the latent
scores are rank-deficient, while still avoiding test-set leakage.

\paragraph{Calibration metrics.}
We report balanced accuracy, macro-F1, log-loss, and expected
calibration error (ECE) following~\cite{guo2017calibration,
naeini2015obtaining}.

\subsection{Statistical comparison}
We follow the recommendations
of~\cite{demsar2006statistical, benavoli2017time}: per-dataset paired
ranks, Friedman test, Nemenyi post-hoc, and a critical-difference
diagram. Per-pair $t$-statistics use the Nadeau-Bengio corrected
variance~\cite{nadeau2003inference} when the same training set is
re-used across seeds. Headline figures are accompanied by signed
differences against the canonical baselines: $\Delta_{\PLS}=
\mathrm{RMSEP}_{\mathrm{var}}-\mathrm{RMSEP}_{\PLS}$ and
$\Delta_{\mathrm{TabPFN-opt}}$.

\subsection{Leakage safety}
The protocol is leakage-safe at three levels: (i) operator selection
uses only training-fold information; (ii) the logistic calibrator is
trained on the same fold; (iii) the test set is touched once per
variant per dataset to compute final metrics. The reproducibility
appendix lists exact commands, seeds, and code versions.

\section{Regression benchmark results}\label{sec:results-regression}
%% =====================================================================

The regression benchmark answers three messages, in this order. First,
the deployed production \AOM-\PLS is the reference, not a competitor:
the \AOM\_v0 framework reproduces its predictions within
floating-point tolerance on the parity cohort
(Section~\ref{sec:bit-parity}), so any difference that
appears in the cohort tables for the
\verb|nirs4all-AOM-PLS-default| variant is a property of the production
estimator itself, not of the framework wrapper. Second,
the strongest operator-adaptive variants win against standard \PLS on
the majority of NIRS regression splits in our cohort. Third, the
TabPFN comparison is sobering: compact-bank \AOM and \textsc{cv}-3 are
competitive with TabPFN-Raw, CatBoost, and CNN on meaningful subsets,
but TabPFN-opt remains ahead overall.

\paragraph{Non-linear preprocessing in front of \AOM is the new

at $1.008$, 28/57). \emph{Direct} \OSC (Orthogonal Signal Correction
with $K{=}2$ orthogonal components, fitted on the full training set
with $y$ available, replayed at predict time) is the most aggressive
preprocessing in the bank â€” and the only one that hurts uniformly:
median ratios of $1.347$ and $1.350$ for \texttt{OSC-AOM-default} and
\texttt{OSC-AOM-compact}, with only 14/57 and 13/57 wins. The
\SNV+\OSC and \MSC+\OSC composites mitigate part of the damage
($1.318$--$1.320$, 15/57) but stay above $1.0$. The diagnosis is that
$K{=}2$ removes too much $y$-related variance on small-$n$ splits
because the orthogonal directions are estimated from the same $y$
that drives the supervised \PLS solve, leading to an effective
double-dip; auto-selecting \OSC's $K$ jointly with the operator (left
to future work) would close the gap.

holdout.} Replacing the deterministic single 20\% holdout
(\texttt{RandomState(42)}) with 3-fold \textsc{cv} on the same compact
bank yields \texttt{AOM-compact-cv3} at median $0.997$ and 32/57 wins
â€” a small but consistent improvement over the holdout's
$0.999 / 29$ that is exactly what classical multiple-comparison theory
predicts when one trades a rank-1 noise estimate for a rank-3 estimate
on the same nine candidates. Approximate \textsc{press} on the same
bank performs worse ($1.019$, 24/57) because the leverage adjustment
is unreliable on small-$n$ splits; the deployed deterministic holdout
sits in between, which justifies its continued use as the production
default while signalling \textsc{cv}-3 as the easiest engineering win.

wins on the majority of splits with the right bank.}
Counting per-dataset wins against \PLS-standard, \texttt{SNV-AOM-compact}

\texttt{nirs4all-AOM-PLS} 29/57, \texttt{AOM-default-nipals-adjoint}
29/57 (production-parity replica), \texttt{AOM-default-simpls-covariance} 28/57,
\texttt{ActiveSuperblock} 28/57, \texttt{MSC-AOM-compact} 28/57,

when \POP is constrained to fewer components.

\paragraph{Active Superblock and the operator explorer track the
deployed reference.}
The Active Superblock variant (median $1.004$, 28/57 wins) and the
operator explorer variant (median $1.000$, 24/57 wins) sit within
selection-noise of the production \AOM-\PLS reference at the median,
and contribute different preprocessing combinations on individual
splits. This is consistent with the design intent: both modes wrap
the parity-validated \AOM core but allow multiple preprocessing views
to contribute jointly. The bank-screening step in Active Superblock
($-\|\Amat_b S\|_F$ ranking, response-cosine diversity, Frobenius
block balancing) is what stops the raw multi-view \SIMPLS baseline
from being dominated by high-gain derivative operators. The operator
explorer side-steps the same problem by exposing only a small,
beam-searched, diverse set of composed operators to the selector.

\verb|delta_rmsep_vs_tabpfn_raw| and

claim that any \AOM variant beats TabPFN-opt overall, but the gap
narrows from $1.163$ (production) to $1.117$ (champion) and the
per-dataset wins climb from 11/57 to 18/57.

\subsection{Floating-point parity with production AOM-PLS}\label{sec:bit-parity}

The \AOM\_v0 framework is parity-validated against the deployed
\verb|nirs4all.operators.models.sklearn.aom_pls.AOMPLSRegressor|.
``Parity'' here means three properties: the framework's selected
operator index $b^\star$ is the same as production's, its selected
component count $k^\star$ is the same, and the resulting test-set RMSEP
agrees up to floating-point noise (numerical tolerance $10^{-4}$;
typically the difference is exactly $0.0$). Parity is verified as an
\emph{automated regression test}, not as a benchmark observation, by
\verb|bench/AOM_v0/tests/test_parity_with_production.py| over the
following 11-dataset cohort:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=0pt]
  \item \texttt{BEER/Beer\_OriginalExtract\_60\_KS} ($k^\star{=}1$,
        RMSEP $0.2042$)
  \item \texttt{CORN/Corn\_Oil\_80\_ZhengChenPelegYbaseSplit}
        ($k^\star{=}15$, RMSEP $0.0222$)
  \item \texttt{CORN/Corn\_Starch\_80\_ZhengChenPelegYbaseSplit}
        ($k^\star{=}11$, RMSEP $0.1695$)
  \item \texttt{PHOSPHORUS/LP\_spxyG} ($k^\star{=}14$, RMSEP $0.1728$)
  \item \texttt{IncombustibleMaterial/TIC\_spxy70} ($k^\star{=}12$,
        RMSEP $3.0921$)
  \item \texttt{PLUMS/Firmness\_spxy70} ($k^\star{=}7$, RMSEP $0.3771$)
  \item \texttt{PEACH/Brix\_spxy70} ($k^\star{=}15$, RMSEP $2.0182$)
  \item \texttt{AMYLOSE/Rice\_Amylose\_313\_YbasedSplit}
        ($k^\star{=}13$, RMSEP $1.8873$)
  \item \texttt{BISCUIT/Biscuit\_Sucrose\_40\_RandomSplit}
        ($k^\star{=}15$, RMSEP $4.3469$)
  \item \texttt{BISCUIT/Biscuit\_Fat\_40\_RandomSplit} ($k^\star{=}8$,
        RMSEP $0.5491$)
  \item \texttt{MILK/Milk\_Lactose\_1224\_KS} ($k^\star{=}15$, RMSEP
        $0.0580$)
\end{itemize}

The cohort is deliberately diverse: small-$n$ versus large-$n$,
low-noise versus noisy, derivative-friendly versus scatter-friendly.
Every entry passes the parity assertions on every commit.

\paragraph{Six parity-critical implementation details.}
Achieving strict parity required six specific implementation decisions
to be aligned with the production reference. We document them here as
the \emph{reproducibility surface} of the benchmark; they are the
points of failure if a future commit or an independent reimplementation
diverges:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Identity is auto-prepended} to every user-supplied
        operator bank. Production uses
        \verb|[Identity, ...user_ops]|; \AOM\_v0 must do the same so
        that selection has access to the no-op direction.
  \item \textbf{Holdout RNG is \texttt{numpy.random.RandomState(42)}},
        not \verb|np.random.default_rng|. The two RNGs draw different
        permutations even with identical seeds: production uses the
        legacy MT19937 \verb|RandomState(42)|, and the framework copies
        that exactly. With the small-$n$ holdouts in the cohort, the
        choice of permutation materially shifts the held-out subset
        and changes the selected $k^\star$.
  \item \textbf{Holdout size is $n_\text{ho}=\max(3, n/5)$} (a single
        split, no per-fold re-centering). Production centres the
        training matrix once globally and never re-centres inside the
        holdout split.
  \item \textbf{The \NIPALS-adjoint algorithm uses production's exact
        four-step weight update}
        $g=\Amat^\top c \to \hat{\wvec}=g/\|g\| \to a_w=\Amat\hat{\wvec}
        \to \wvec=a_w/\|a_w\|$ (Section~\ref{sec:algo-nipals-adj}).
        Any algebraic shortcut --- including the literal \SIMPLS-style
        $\wvec\propto\Amat^\top\Amat\,c$ update --- breaks parity for
        non-commuting compositions in the default 100-operator bank.
  \item \textbf{The Norris-Williams $d{=}2$ kernel} is defined as
        $\text{gap\_kernel}\circledast\text{gap\_kernel}$, a length
        $4g{+}1$ kernel obtained by convolving the gap-derivative
        kernel with itself. A naive \texttt{[1/g\^{}2,\,-2/g\^{}2,\,1/g\^{}2]}
        kernel of length $2g{+}1$ is mathematically tempting but
        produces different filter coefficients and breaks parity.
  \item \textbf{Shape-safe residual subtraction inside holdout
        scoring.} Production's $y_\text{val}$ is a 1-D vector and the
        residual is a 2-D column; \AOM\_v0 used to broadcast the
        wrong way and silently drift on PLS1 datasets. The fix is a
        single \verb|.reshape(-1)| before the subtraction.
\end{enumerate}

These six items, plus the \texttt{default} bank composition described
in Section~\ref{sec:framework}, fully define the parity surface. The
parity test sanity-checks the bank size (must be exactly 100 entries,
matching \verb|nirs4all.default_operator_bank|) on every run.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Production \AOM-\PLS as the deployed reference.}
The framework's empirical conclusions rest on the production-parity
property of Section~\ref{sec:bit-parity}. The deployed
\verb|nirs4all.AOMPLSRegressor| is not a competitor in our benchmark;
it is the source of truth that the framework's
\texttt{AOM-default-nipals-adjoint} run reproduces on the parity
cohort. Practical consequence:
when a future commit accidentally breaks parity, the regression test
fires immediately; when a benchmark line that uses
\verb|nirs4all-AOM-PLS-default| moves on a future cohort, the move is
attributable to the deployed estimator, not to the framework wrapper.
This separation is what makes the multi-view extensions
(Section~\ref{sec:algo-active-superblock},
Section~\ref{sec:algo-explorer}) honest research extensions rather
than redesigns of a moving target.

\paragraph{When does \AOM help, in production?}
\AOM-\textsc{global} reduces the analyst's burden by replacing an
external preprocessing grid with a single training-time choice on a
bank of strict linear operators. On the 57 complete comparisons, the
deployed \verb|nirs4all-AOM-PLS-default| is essentially tied with
standard \PLS at the median (relative RMSEP $0.999$) while winning on
29/57 splits. The clearest gains come from reducing selection variance
or adding a scatter correction outside the strict-linear bank:
\texttt{AOM-compact-cv3} reaches $0.997$ with 32/57 wins, and
\texttt{SNV-AOM-compact} reaches $0.984$ with 32/57 wins. This pattern
suggests that the current production default is conservative but
selector-limited: the 100-operator bank is useful as a library of
chemometric filters, yet a single 20\% holdout is too noisy to choose
among $100\times K_\text{max}$ candidate operator/component pairs on
many small NIRS splits.

\paragraph{Active Superblock and the operator explorer as research
extensions.}
Active Superblock is the multi-view counterpart of \AOM-\textsc{global}:
instead of selecting one operator, it gives every retained operator a
voice but enforces diversity (response-cosine pruning at $0.98$) and
balanced amplitude (Frobenius scaling) up front. The operator explorer
goes further by treating the bank itself as a search target, beam-
searching composition chains in covariance space using a probe-based
gain proxy. Both extensions stay inside the strict-linear operator
contract --- they manipulate the bank, not the extraction algorithm
--- so their predictions are linear functions of the input spectra
just like standard \PLS, and the production-parity property of the
underlying \AOM core is preserved when the bank reduces to a single
operator.

\paragraph{When does \POP help?}
\POP allows the operator to vary across components.
Figure~\ref{fig:operator-paths} contrasts the operator paths chosen by
\AOM-\textsc{global} (a flat selection: identity for every component)
and \POP (a non-trivial path that mixes operators across components)
on a synthetic regression dataset. On the 57-dataset cohort \POP does
not justify itself as a default: both compact-bank \POP rows reach only
13/57 wins against \PLS, and the deployed \texttt{nirs4all-POP-PLS}
row is unstable at $K_\text{max}=15$. The failure mode is informative,
not merely negative: changing operators at every component increases
the effective search space and needs an explicit path-regularisation
or early-stopping rule. The mechanism remains auditable: every selected
operator index is recorded in
\verb|selected_operator_sequence_json| and every per-component score
in \verb|selected_operator_scores_json|.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{../figures/fig_operator_paths.pdf}
\caption{Operator paths selected by \AOM-\textsc{global} (blue) and
\POP (red) on a synthetic regression dataset. The vertical axis lists
the bank entries; the horizontal axis is the latent component index.
\AOM picks one operator for all components; \POP picks an operator at
each component independently.}
\label{fig:operator-paths}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{When does identity win?}
For datasets whose chemistry is already encoded in the dominant
covariance direction, identity is selected and \AOM degenerates to
\PLS. We treat this as evidence that the framework is conservative: it
does not introduce spurious operators, and operator paths that contain
identity-only entries are valid outputs.

\paragraph{Covariance-space \SIMPLS as conceptual clarifier.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:central-identity} reframes preprocessing selection
as a linear pre-multiplication on the cross-covariance. The
covariance-space \SIMPLS engine is the natural consequence for a
\emph{strict} \SIMPLS reading of the problem ($\wvec\propto\Amat^\top
\Amat\,c$). The deployed estimator and the parity-validated
\NIPALS-adjoint engine use a different update (the four-step adjoint
flow of Section~\ref{sec:algo-nipals-adj}); the two flows agree for
symmetric and anti-symmetric operators but disagree for general
non-commuting compositions. The \SIMPLS-covariance engine is therefore
useful as a fast strict-\SIMPLS implementation and as a conceptual
companion to the production \NIPALS-adjoint engine, not as a
drop-in replacement for it.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
%% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Production parity defines the framework's reproducibility surface.}
The six implementation details enumerated in Section~\ref{sec:bit-parity}
--- identity auto-prepend, MT19937 \verb|RandomState(42)| holdout RNG,
$n_\text{ho}=\max(3, n/5)$ no-re-centering split, the four-step
\NIPALS-adjoint weight update, the Norris-Williams $d{=}2$ kernel
defined as $\text{gap}\circledast\text{gap}$, and shape-safe residual
subtraction --- are not features. They are the surface on which the
benchmark's reproducibility rests. An independent reimplementation
that gets any one of them wrong will deviate from the production
reference and therefore from every empirical claim in
Section~\ref{sec:results-regression} that involves
\verb|nirs4all-AOM-PLS-default| or the framework's
\texttt{AOM-default-nipals-adjoint} variant. The parity test is the canonical
documentation of these choices.

\paragraph{The parity cohort covers 11 datasets, the benchmark table covers 57.}
Production parity is asserted on 11 deterministic regression splits:
large enough to cover diverse operator choices and small enough to run
as a routine regression test. The headline benchmark table covers 57
complete all-variant comparisons. The parity test is therefore a
guardrail for the production-aligned implementation, not a claim that
every one of the 57 splits is independently tested against the deployed
estimator on every commit.

\paragraph{SNV and MSC are not strict linear operators.}
SNV row-standardisation~\cite{barnes1989standard} and
MSC~\cite{geladi1985linearization} apply parameters that depend on the
row being transformed. They are therefore not fixed-matrix operators
and do not satisfy identity~\eqref{eq:central-identity}. In our
framework SNV/MSC must remain external preprocessors applied
\emph{before} \PLS, exactly as in standard chemometric practice. A
linearised SNV (centred per row but with a fixed scale factor learned
from training data) would satisfy the identity but would be a
different operator and would behave differently on test data; we do
not introduce that variant in this paper.

\paragraph{Strict-\SIMPLS and production-\NIPALS disagree off the
symmetric subset.}
Section~\ref{sec:algo-nipals-adj} contrasts the production-aligned
four-step \NIPALS-adjoint update with a strict-\SIMPLS reading
$\wvec\propto\Amat^\top\Amat\,c$. The two flows produce identical
predictions whenever $\Amat$ is symmetric or anti-symmetric. They do
\emph{not} agree for general non-commuting compositions, and a number
of operators in the default 100-operator bank fall in that regime
(notably composed savgol-then-detrend operators and Norris-Williams
gap-derivative kernels). The covariance-space \SIMPLS engine is
therefore an auxiliary, not a replacement: every parity-validated
benchmark line in Section~\ref{sec:results-regression} that involves
the production reference uses the four-step \NIPALS-adjoint engine.

\paragraph{Soft mixture degeneracy.}
The convex combination policy is included for completeness but
collapses to hard \textsc{global} selection under the covariance
objective (Supplement~A.3). It is therefore exposed as a research
interface, not as a recommended default.

\paragraph{Classification benchmark not yet complete.}
The \PLSDA extension is implemented, but the local classification run
is only a smoke test. It exposed an SVD non-convergence on a
high-class-imbalance dataset when the residualised cross-covariance
became rank deficient. This is a numerical robustness issue in the
classification path; it is logged as an exception row rather than
papered over, and no classification performance claim is made here.

\paragraph{No claim of universal superiority over TabPFN, and no
claim of algorithmic novelty for \AOM-\PLS itself.}
The deployed \nirsfourall \AOM-\PLS estimator is the algorithm we
re-implement and validate; we do not claim novelty for it. We do not
claim \AOM/\POP \PLS beats TabPFN-opt on the master cohort. What we
claim is a parity-validated framework wrapper on top of which Active
Superblock, the operator explorer, and the deep banks can be evaluated
against the deployed reference under one mathematical contract and one
leakage-safe protocol.

Result CSVs share a stable schema (see \verb|RESULT_COLUMNS| in
\verb|run_aompls_benchmark.py|) including diagnostic columns
\verb|selected_operator_sequence_json| and
\verb|selected_operator_scores_json|, which let downstream readers
audit every operator decision per fold per dataset.

%% =====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%% =====================================================================

Operator-Adaptive \PLS turns spectral preprocessing selection from an
external grid into an internal, auditable component of \PLS. The
deployed \nirsfourall \verb|AOMPLSRegressor| is the reference
implementation; this paper supplies a parity-validated framework
wrapper that reproduces its predictions within floating-point tolerance
on an 11-dataset regression cohort, documents the six implementation details that
define the parity surface, and uses that wrapper to evaluate three
research extensions. Active Superblock and the operator explorer are
included in the 57-dataset cohort joined to the published TabPFN/NIRS
reference results, while deep order-3/order-4 banks are kept as a
separate capacity ablation. Standard \PLS, \AOM-\textsc{global},
\POP, and the multi-view modes share a single mathematical contract
anchored on the identity
$(\Xmat\Amat^\top)^\top \Ymat = \Amat\,\Xmat^\top \Ymat$. Empirically,
the strongest variants (\texttt{SNV-AOM-compact} and
\texttt{AOM-compact-cv3}) beat standard \PLS on 32/57 splits, while the
production \AOM-\PLS reference remains a near-tie at the median
($0.999$ relative RMSEP, 29/57 wins). TabPFN-opt remains ahead overall,
so the immediate algorithmic lesson is not to add more operators, but
to reduce selection variance, expose scatter correction safely, and
regularise per-component selection. The framework extends to \PLSDA via
class-balanced coding and a leakage-safe logistic calibrator on latent
scores, but the full classification benchmark remains future work. We
do not claim algorithmic novelty for \AOM-\PLS itself, and we do not
claim universal superiority over modern tabular models. What we offer
is a parity-validated, auditable, operator-aware extension of \PLS that
fits naturally inside chemometric pipelines and can be evaluated
against TabPFN, Catboost, and modern deep tabular models under a
single, reproducible protocol.
